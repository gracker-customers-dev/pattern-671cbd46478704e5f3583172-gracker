Tax liens

A legal claim by a government against a property to secure payment of taxes owed.

Description

A tax lien is a legal right or interest that a government entity has in an individual's property until the tax debt obligation is satisfied. When a property owner fails to pay taxes, the government can place a lien on the property as a way to ensure the collection of owed taxes. This lien serves as a public notice that the government has a claim against the property, which must be settled before the property can be sold or refinanced. Tax liens can also affect the property owner's credit score and may lead to foreclosure if unpaid. In many jurisdictions, after a certain period, tax liens can be sold to third parties who then have the right to collect the owed taxes, often with interest. This can create opportunities for investors to buy tax lien certificates, which can yield returns if the property owner pays off the lien. Overall, tax liens are a critical tool for governments to enforce tax collection and can have significant implications for property owners.

Examples

  • A homeowner fails to pay property taxes for three years, resulting in a tax lien on their property.
  • An investor purchases a tax lien certificate at a tax lien auction, gaining the right to collect owed taxes plus interest.

Additional Information

  • Tax liens can vary by state, with different rules regarding interest rates and redemption periods.
  • Investing in tax liens can be an alternative investment strategy with potential returns, but it carries risks.
