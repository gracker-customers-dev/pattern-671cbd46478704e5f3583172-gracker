Loan contingency

A stipulation in a contract that makes the agreement contingent upon the approval of a loan.

Description

In real estate transactions, a loan contingency is a clause that allows the buyer to back out of the purchase agreement if they are unable to secure a mortgage loan within a specified period. This provides a level of protection for the buyer, ensuring that they are not obligated to proceed with the purchase if financing cannot be obtained. Typically, the buyer must apply for the loan within a certain timeframe and provide evidence of their efforts to secure financing. If the loan is denied, the buyer can withdraw from the agreement without penalty, allowing them to recover their earnest money deposit. Conversely, if the loan contingency is satisfied and the buyer secures financing, they are then obligated to proceed with the purchase. This mechanism is crucial in protecting buyers from financial risks associated with acquiring property, especially in a fluctuating market where loan approvals can be unpredictable.

Examples

  • A buyer includes a loan contingency in their offer to purchase a home, allowing them to cancel if financing falls through.
  • A seller accepts an offer with a loan contingency, understanding that the buyer's purchase is dependent on loan approval.

Additional Information

  • Loan contingencies typically have a set time frame, often ranging from 30 to 60 days.
  • Buyers should be prepared to provide documentation to their lenders promptly to avoid delays in the approval process.
