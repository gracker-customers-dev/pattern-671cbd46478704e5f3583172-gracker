Adjustable rate mortgages

A type of mortgage where the interest rate can change periodically based on a benchmark rate.

Description

Adjustable rate mortgages (ARMs) are home loans where the interest rate is not fixed and can vary at predetermined intervals, usually in relation to an index rate such as the LIBOR or the U.S. Treasury rate. Initially, ARMs often come with a lower interest rate compared to fixed-rate mortgages, which can make them attractive to borrowers seeking lower initial payments. However, after an initial fixed period, the interest rate adjusts periodically, leading to potential increases in monthly payments over time. This variability can make it challenging for borrowers to budget for future payments. ARMs are typically structured with caps that limit how much the interest rate can increase at each adjustment and over the life of the loan. Borrowers need to evaluate their financial situation and risk tolerance before choosing an ARM, as market fluctuations can significantly affect their total loan cost.

Examples

  • 5/1 ARM: Fixed for the first 5 years, then adjusts annually.
  • 7/1 ARM: Fixed for the first 7 years, then adjusts annually.

Additional Information

  • ARMs can lead to lower initial payments compared to fixed-rate mortgages.
  • Borrowers should review the terms carefully to understand how and when rates will adjust.
