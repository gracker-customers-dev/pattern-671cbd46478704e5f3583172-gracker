Capital gains tax

A tax on the profit from the sale of an asset.

Description

Capital gains tax is a levy imposed on the profit realized from the sale of a non-inventory asset, such as stocks, bonds, real estate, or other investments. The tax is calculated based on the difference between the purchase price (cost basis) and the selling price of the asset. In many jurisdictions, capital gains are classified into short-term and long-term gains, with short-term gains (from assets held for one year or less) typically taxed at higher ordinary income tax rates, whereas long-term gains (from assets held for more than one year) are taxed at reduced rates. The application of capital gains tax can influence investment strategies, as investors may hold assets longer to benefit from lower tax rates. Additionally, capital gains tax can impact the economy by affecting consumer spending and investment decisions. Taxpayers are also responsible for reporting capital gains on their tax returns, and there are various exemptions and deductions available depending on the asset and situation. Understanding capital gains tax is crucial for investors to optimize their returns and comply with tax regulations.

Examples

  • Selling a house for a profit after owning it for several years may trigger long-term capital gains tax.
  • An investor selling stocks after holding them for less than a year will incur short-term capital gains tax.

Additional Information

  • Capital gains tax rates vary by country and can change over time.
  • Some jurisdictions offer exemptions, such as for primary residences or specific types of investments.
