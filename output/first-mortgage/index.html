First mortgage

A first mortgage is the primary loan secured against a property, giving the lender the first claim on the asset in case of default.

Description

A first mortgage is a legal agreement between a borrower and a lender, where the lender provides funds to the borrower to purchase a property, and in return, the lender holds a lien on the property. This lien gives the lender the primary right to recover the loan amount by selling the property if the borrower fails to repay the loan. First mortgages typically have lower interest rates compared to second mortgages because they are less risky for lenders. The amount of the first mortgage is usually determined by the appraised value of the property and the borrower’s creditworthiness. It is important for homeowners to understand that a first mortgage takes precedence over any other loans secured against the property, meaning that in a foreclosure situation, the first mortgage lender will be paid off before any other creditors. Borrowers can refinance their first mortgages to secure better rates or terms, which can lead to lower monthly payments or access to equity.

Examples

  • A homeowner obtains a first mortgage to buy a new house, allowing them to finance a significant portion of the purchase price.
  • A property developer secures a first mortgage to acquire land for a new construction project, leveraging the property as collateral.

Additional Information

  • First mortgages are typically long-term loans with repayment periods ranging from 15 to 30 years.
  • The interest paid on a first mortgage may be tax-deductible, depending on local tax laws and the borrower's financial situation.
