Capital gain tax

A tax on the profit from the sale of an asset.

Description

Capital gain tax refers to the tax levied on the profit realized from the sale of a capital asset, such as stocks, bonds, or real estate. When an asset is sold for more than its purchase price, the difference is considered a capital gain and is subject to taxation. The tax rate may vary based on the holding period of the asset; short-term gains (for assets held for one year or less) are typically taxed at a higher ordinary income tax rate, while long-term gains (for assets held for more than one year) are taxed at a lower rate. The implications of capital gain tax can influence investment strategies, as investors may hold onto assets longer to benefit from lower tax rates. Additionally, capital gain tax can be affected by various factors, including the investor's income level and specific tax laws in different jurisdictions. Understanding capital gain tax is essential for effective financial planning and investment decision-making.

Examples

  • Selling a stock for $10,000 after buying it for $7,000 results in a capital gain of $3,000.
  • Selling a property for $300,000 after purchasing it for $200,000 incurs a capital gain of $100,000.

Additional Information

  • Capital losses can be used to offset capital gains, reducing taxable income.
  • Certain exemptions and deductions may apply, such as the primary residence exclusion for real estate.
