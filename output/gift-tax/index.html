Gift tax

A federal tax imposed on the transfer of property or money from one individual to another without receiving something of equal value in return.

Description

Gift tax is a tax applied to the transfer of money or property from one individual to another without compensation. This tax is intended to prevent individuals from avoiding estate taxes by giving away assets while they are still alive. In the United States, the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) allows individuals to give a certain amount each year, known as the annual exclusion, without incurring gift tax. As of 2023, this amount is $17,000 per recipient. Gifts above this amount may be subject to gift tax, which is calculated based on the value of the gift, and the donor is responsible for paying the tax. Additionally, there is a lifetime exemption amount that allows individuals to give away a larger sum over their lifetime without incurring gift tax. Gift tax regulations can be complex and vary by jurisdiction, making it important for individuals to understand their obligations when making significant gifts.

Examples

  • A grandparent gives $20,000 to their grandchild for college expenses, exceeding the annual exclusion amount.
  • A parent transfers ownership of a rental property valued at $200,000 to their child without charging rent.

Additional Information

  • Gift tax rates can range from 18% to 40%, depending on the total amount of taxable gifts made.
  • Certain gifts, such as tuition payments made directly to an educational institution or medical expenses paid directly to a provider, are exempt from gift tax.
